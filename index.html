How we actually operate
Hugo bills top-down against contracted capacity. That's our default, and it's how most BPO engagements are structured. But billing is not our commitment to you. Our operating standard is to staff at least 10-15% above contracted headcount on every workflow - a cushion for PTO, shrinkage, attrition variance, ramp unevenness. That buffer exists so that total hours delivered always exceed invoiced hours. Delivered above billed is the real promise.
The structure in our MSA - and the billing habits we've built around it - was designed for a smaller account where numbers were easy to audit, and where our buffer discipline guaranteed delivered hours were always comfortably above contracted. It's not that we don't have raw data. We have the same Assembled exports, the same Penmac payroll, the same training records that you do. We've just never used raw data as the basis for billing - we use it internally to validate that our top-down position is correct. The raw data for Feb and March confirms it: every workflow, both months, delivered above billed.
That said, the account has grown, the workflows are more complex, and our old cadence wasn't giving you enough visibility for you to independently audit us. That's what's changing.
What we got wrong this year
Two things compressed the margin between delivered and billed. Both are on us.
1. Gastro buffer was cut at end of 2025. As part of an internal efficiency push we moved Gastro from its usual ~15% buffer down to roughly 4% (47 actual agents against 45 contracted). This was a Hugo-side decision driven by margin pressure. Where we erred was not recognizing that once buffers drop below a certain level, the top-down billing method stops being the right approach. Top-down works cleanly when buffers are thick - delivered is always comfortably above billed, raw data confirms it, and the invoice is simple. Once buffers tighten, the right move is to transition that workflow to bottom-up billing backed by raw data, not to keep running top-down with a thinner margin for error. We didn't make that transition on Gastro when we should have.
2. Missouri attrition was running roughly 2x our initial estimate. When we saw the gap, we deliberately spent time diagnosing the root cause rather than acting fast, refilling buffers, and ending up in the same situation a month later. That took several weeks of data-gathering and diagnosis. We've landed on a set of specific solutions (recruiting pipeline changes, training model adjustments, retention levers) that we'd like to walk through with you as a separate conversation next week. The short version: we understand what's driving it and we have a plan.
One more honest reflection: our internal tracking was focused on aggregate account-level delivery - total delivered vs total billed, across all workflows. That aggregate has looked healthy throughout the year and still does (Feb +7,275 hrs, March +6,684 hrs above billed). But a workstream-by-workstream view would have flagged Gastro running close to the bone before you did. That's a reporting-discipline gap - we were looking at the right number at the wrong level - and it's part of what the new cadence fixes.
A note on the billing approach Cris and Ross proposed Friday
Separate from the three concerns above, Cris and Ross walked through a specific billing method on Friday that's worth addressing directly. We want to make sure we understood it correctly, and if we did, explain why we see it differently. If we misunderstood, this is an invitation to clarify.
As we understood it: start with scheduled hours, subtract PTO and unscheduled absences to get delivered production hours, then prorate delivered hours by the ratio of contracted to billable agents (e.g., 45 contracted / 47 billable = 95.7%). The prorated number is what Hugo should bill.
Why this doesn't work as a billing framework:
1. There are no "named" contracted vs buffer agents at Hugo. All 47 Gastro agents are cross-trained, interchangeably rostered, and deliver the same work product. Agent 46 isn't second-class - they handle the same tickets as agent 1. Prorating by the ratio only makes mathematical sense if buffer agents could be logically excluded from contracted output, but that's not how our operating model works. We don't name agents to slots - we name all of them as production agents precisely so there's always coverage.
2. The method accepts output from buffer agents then declines to pay for it. GH accepted the work product from all 47 agents. Tickets got resolved, SLAs got hit, customer escalations got handled. Asymmetric acceptance - take the output, decline the capacity - isn't a viable commercial framework. The symmetry test: Hugo bills contracted (45), no more. When delivered is above contracted, GH gets extra capacity at no charge (that's buffer working in GH's favor). When delivered dips near contracted, GH still pays contracted, because that's what was booked. The asymmetry already goes in GH's favor, not Hugo's.
If the concern is that 47 billable means Hugo is trying to bill for 47, that's not what's happening. The invoice is always for the contracted 45 (or wherever contracted sits for a given workflow). Billable count just reflects Hugo's staffing decision to carry buffer - it's an operational signal, not a billing number. The billing framework is clean: Hugo invoices contracted capacity, staffs above contracted, and absorbs the buffer cost.
That said, the Friday confusion on this point is itself a signal that the framework isn't as transparent as it should be. That's on us to fix (see commitments below).

This is a trust problem. Here's how we rebuild it.
The bigger signal in Friday's conversation isn't whether a specific workflow was billed right. It's that we haven't been doing enough to build the kind of trust where you don't have to ask. Not showing our work leaves a lot of room for interpretation, and interpretation can go a long way from where we sit - in some cases to places that question our integrity. That's not acceptable, and it's not where we want this relationship to be.
Moving forward, we're committing to being substantially more transparent - raw data and methodology both. Eventually we'd like to explore API-level access to our Assembled and CRM data so your team can audit in real time without waiting for our monthly cadence. In the meantime, here's what we're putting in place immediately:
  • Weekly reconciliation, tapering to monthly over time.
    Starting immediately: weekly recon of raw data (scheduled, attended, productive, by workflow) flowing up into a monthly invoice packet. Once the cadence is stable and both teams are confident in the numbers, we taper to biweekly, then back to monthly. A weekly audit beat at the start is good practice for rebuilding visibility.
  • More detailed invoices, every month.
    Every billed hour walks down to individual agent, by workflow, with rates and amounts. This portal is the reference standard - every invoice ships with one like it by the 5th of the following month. No more line-item-only invoices.
  • Transition to bottom-up billing when buffers fall below threshold.
    Our top-down billing method works cleanly when buffers are comfortably above contracted - delivered always exceeds billed and raw data confirms it. When buffers tighten below a defined threshold (our starting point: 10%), we transition that workflow to bottom-up billing backed by raw data. This gives both sides a clear, pre-agreed trigger and removes the ambiguity that surfaced on Gastro this quarter.
  • Training folded into Assembled (request to GH).
    Today, Assembled tracks production-only, so training hours don't have the same visibility. We'd like GH's team to enable training-workflow tracking in Assembled so training and training attendance are captured alongside production. Same transparency, same auditability. This is a lift on your side but it closes a real reporting gap.
  • Missouri staffing overhaul, detail coming next week.
    We've worked through specific solutions for MO that we'd like to walk through with you as a separate conversation next week. The short version: we understand what's driving the attrition gap and we have a plan.

Key terms & billing definitions
Maximum contracted hours
Max hours Hugo can bill. Billable agents × working days × hrs/day.
Training hours
Hours in ~2-week pre-production training. NOT tracked in Assembled.
Production hours
Hours contracted agents spend in production after training. Tracked in Assembled.
Total invoiced hours
Training (contracted) + Production (contracted) + TLs + Other + OT. What the client pays for.
Assembled hours
Hours in Assembled (WFM tool). Production only. Includes contracted AND buffer. Includes overtime.
Buffer agents
Extra agents for SLA coverage. Never invoiced. Production appears in Assembled. Hugo absorbs cost.
Scheduled (adjusted)
Total Hours from Assembled MINUS PTO, LOA, holiday, vacation. The "paid-for production time" number.
Attended
Scheduled MINUS same-day absences (NCNS, late arrival, early departure). Hours agents actually worked.
Productive
Attended MINUS breaks, meals, meetings, coaching, readiness. Hours on-task delivering value.
Overtime hours
Extra hours requested by client. Billed at ~20% premium.
Weekend hours
Weekend shifts billed at 1.2x premium. Added from March 2026 after compliance review.
Completed training
Training hours billed to GH reflect agents who completed training. Drop-outs are not invoiced.
